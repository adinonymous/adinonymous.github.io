The year of 2025 was the year I first began cooking for myself. Now, in my culture, onions are an essential ingredient, to call a meal a complete one.

So let's study this vegetable.


-----

## Composition

Onions are 85-90% water. They've got sulfur (this makes you cry), sugars (polysaccharides like sucrose and monosaccharides like glucose and fructose), and structure keeping the cells rigid.

Cooking an onion is an interplay with these building blocks. 

Each cell in an onion is filled with water and other compounds, and chopping an onion breaks the structural walls (a polysaccharide named pectin) holding these contents together.

So sulfur is released, and chemical reactions start kicking off, including on the surface of your eyes. 

## Heat

Heating defines how the contents of the onion break down.  

### Low Heat: Sweating

Prior to any heat, onion cells have water inside, and rigid walls with some air gaps around. 

At low heats, onions start releasing their water at a cellular level. This softens them, and the heat starts breaking down the cell walls. 

As these cell walls break down, cells lose their shapes. The air gaps around these cell walls also start vanishing, and get filled by water and liquid (dissolved sugars/sulfur).

Now, with the air gaps gone, and mostly fluid/water and broken down cell walls being the majority, the physical boundaries in the cells reduce, and a more uniform medium is created. 

Light can pass through this medium, in thin layers. This is when the translucency starts appearing.

Structurally, this signals the softness of the onion.

### Medium Heat: Browning

Once the water has evaporated, its time for the onions to begin browning. 

Browning in onions happens by 2 simultaneous chemical processes:
1. Caramellization
2. Maillard Reaction

#### Caramellization
Heat enables endothermic reactions. Breaking stable molecules into reactive pieces, enabling more complex reactions. 

This kicks in at ~160° C.

The sugars in the onions: glucose, fructose, sucrose - at these temperatures start to break down into reactive, smaller molecules. 

At this heat, dehydration also kicks in, we are past the 100° C point here. 

The smaller molecules form caramellization product polymers. 

#### Maillard Reaction
The maillard reaction is a chemical process that happens even across other common foods, and involves sugars and amino acids (proteins). 

This process kicks in at ~140° C, and gets going between 150-180° C. 

I tried reading up on this but it gets increasingly chemical past the point of intuitiveness.

From a first principles perspective, the summary is - amino acids and sugars, under heat, combine to form an unstable intermediate compound, which begins breaking down into amines and other compounds, which kick off a chain of reactions - each creating a complex flavor.

Water, consequently, can dilute reactants and lower temperature -- thereby slowing down browning. And so the dehydration accelerates browning.

Conversely, since we're dealing with amines, a basic environment/higher pH will accelerate Maillard reaction. So adding baking soda increases browning.

What's interesting is that, at this stage, sometimes these brown bits start sticking to the base of your pan. Removing them is just a matter of adding a bit of water, since these compounds are all water-soluble. So hydrate and spread around again for even flavor.

### Extended Medium Heat: Deep Caramellization
At low-medium heat for an extended duration, when onions break down to near paste-like consistency, the sugars have undergone extensive reactions and polymerization.

## With Acids

The reason people add a sprinkle of lime extract, or a slice of lime, alongside onion-based dishes like egg fried rice, or biryani, is because the acidity from the lime helps keeps the pectin stabilized. 

Recall that pectin is what the onion cell walls are made of. The acidity helps preserve the crispiness of the onions.
